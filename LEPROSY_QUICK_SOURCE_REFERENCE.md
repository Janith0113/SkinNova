## 🎯 Top 5 Essential Sources (Start Here)

### 1. **WHO Leprosy Hub** 
- **URL**: https://www.who.int/health-topics/leprosy
- **Best For**: Disease overview, MDT protocols, global guidelines
- **Update Frequency**: Quarterly
- **Key Content**: Classification, treatment regimens, elimination progress

### 2. **CDC Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)**
- **URL**: https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/
- **Best For**: American clinical guidelines, diagnosis, complications
- **Update Frequency**: As needed
- **Key Content**: Treatment guidelines, reaction management, contact tracing

### 3. **CDC Treatment Guidelines** (Official)
- **URL**: https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/diagnosis/treatment-guidelines.html
- **Best For**: Exact MDT drug regimens, dosages, timeline
- **Confidence**: 100% Verified
- **Use This For**: Patient education on treatment

### 4. **International Leprosy Association**
- **URL**: https://www.ilepsyassociation.org/
- **Best For**: Professional standards, classification systems, reactions
- **Access**: Open to members, some public resources
- **Key Content**: Ridley-Jopling classification, reaction types

### 5. **WHO Global Leprosy Report 2024**
- **URL**: https://www.who.int/publications/global-report-on-leprosy-2024
- **Best For**: Latest statistics, trends, case studies
- **Type**: Annual PDF report
- **Credibility**: Gold standard for leprosy epidemiology

## 📋 Essential Data Points Checklist

### For Disease Classification ✅
```
☑ Indeterminate Leprosy (IL)
  Source: WHO Classification
  Key: Usually self-healing, early detection crucial

☑ Tuberculoid (TT)
  Source: WHO, CDC
  Key: Few lesions, strong immunity, good prognosis

☑ Lepromatous (LL)
  Source: WHO, CDC  
  Key: Many lesions, weak immunity, high infectivity

☑ Borderline forms (BT, BB, BL)
  Source: ILA, WHO
  Key: Variable presentation, prone to reactions
```

### For Treatment Protocols ✅
```
☑ Paucibacillary (6 months)
  - Rifampicin 600mg/month
  - Ofloxacin 400mg/day
  - Minocycline 100mg/day
  Source: WHO MDT Guidelines → https://www.who.int/teams/public-health-surveillance-and-response/dpc/ntds/leprosy-elimination/multi-drug-therapy

☑ Multibacillary (12 months)
  - Same drugs, extended duration
  Source: CDC → https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/diagnosis/treatment-guidelines.html
```

### For Reactions ✅
```
☑ Type 1 (Reversal Reaction)
  Timing: During/1 year after treatment
  Treatment: Prednisolone (corticosteroids)
  Source: CDC → https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/complications/reactions.html

☑ Type 2 (ENL - Erythema Nodosum Leprosum)
  Timing: During treatment, up to 10 years
  Treatment: Prednisolone ± Thalidomide
  Source: CDC, WHO Guidelines
```

### For Disability Prevention ✅
```
☑ WHO Disability Grading Scale
  0 = No disability
  1 = Some sensory loss
  2 = Visible deformity/ulceration
  Source: WHO Disability Prevention Framework

☑ Early Detection Targets
  - Diagnosis within 1 year of onset
  - Regular nerve monitoring
  - Prompt reaction management
```

## 💬 Sample AI Training Responses with Citations

### User: "What is leprosy?"

**Response Template:**
```
Leprosy is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium leprae. 
It primarily affects the skin, nerves, and eyes. With early diagnosis and 
appropriate multi-drug therapy (MDT), leprosy is completely curable.

The disease has several forms, ranging from localized (tuberculoid) to 
disseminated (lepromatous), depending on the patient's immune response.

📌 Sources:
- WHO Leprosy Overview: https://www.who.int/health-topics/leprosy
- CDC Leprosy Information: https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/
```

### User: "How is leprosy treated?"

**Response Template:**
```
Leprosy is treated with Multi-Drug Therapy (MDT):

**For Paucibacillary (6 months):**
- Rifampicin 600mg once monthly (supervised)
- Ofloxacin 400mg daily (self-administered)
- Minocycline 100mg daily (self-administered)

**For Multibacillary (12 months):**
Same regimen, extended to 12 months for better bacillary clearance.

Regular monitoring is essential. You become non-infectious within weeks 
of starting treatment.

📌 Sources:
- WHO MDT Guidelines: https://www.who.int/teams/public-health-surveillance-and-response/dpc/ntds/leprosy-elimination/multi-drug-therapy
- CDC Treatment Guidelines: https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/diagnosis/treatment-guidelines.html
```

### User: "What are leprosy reactions?"

**Response Template:**
```
Leprosy reactions are complications that can occur during or after treatment.

**Type 1 Reaction (Reversal Reaction):**
- Triggered by changes in cell-mediated immunity
- Shows as inflammation of existing lesions and/or neuritis
- Treated with corticosteroids
- Can occur during treatment or within 1 year after

**Type 2 Reaction (Erythema Nodosum Leprosum - ENL):**
- Immune complex reaction (more common in BL/LL patients)
- Shows as tender nodules, fever, systemic symptoms
- Treated with corticosteroids and/or thalidomide
- Requires immediate medical attention

Both types are TREATABLE. Report any unusual symptoms to your doctor immediately.

📌 Sources:
- CDC Reactions Guide: https://www.cdc.gov/leprosy/complications/reactions.html
- WHO Clinical Guidelines
- International Leprosy Association Standards
```

## 📊 Confidence Scoring System

Use these scores when training your AI:

```
Very High (95-100%): WHO, CDC, ILA official guidelines
High (85-95%):       WHO/CDC research, peer-reviewed journals
Medium (70-85%):     Secondary sources, expert consensus
Low (50-70%):        Case reports, regional guidelines

Always cite sources with confidence levels > 80% for patient information.
```

## 🔍 How to Verify New Data Before Adding

### 5-Point Verification Checklist:

```
1. ✅ Official Source Check
   - WHO/CDC/ILA publication?
   - Recently updated (< 5 years)?
   - Peer-reviewed?

2. ✅ Cross-Reference Check
   - Does another trusted source confirm it?
   - Are there any conflicting guidelines?
   - Any updates or revisions?

3. ✅ Applicability Check
   - Is it relevant to leprosy care?
   - Appropriate for patient education?
   - Accurate for your region?

4. ✅ Timeline Check
   - Is the information current?
   - Any newer recommendations?
   - Still standard practice?

5. ✅ Source Transparency Check
   - Are authors/organization disclosed?
   - Any conflicts of interest?
   - Reviewed by standardscommittee?
```

## ❌ What NOT to Use

### Unreliable Sources:
- ❌ Wikipedia (it's a start but not authoritative)
- ❌ Generic health websites (WebMD, HealthLine for leprosy)
- ❌ Social media claims or patient testimonials
- ❌ Non-peer-reviewed blogs
- ❌ Outdated materials (> 10 years old for treatment)

### Always Verify:
- Regional variations in treatment
- New clinical trials or guidelines
- Local health authority updates
- Contraindications for specific populations
